How the dashboard is built

Every classification choice that produces the figures on the dashboard is documented here. The goal is reproducibility: a reader should be able to follow the chain from a single FEC Schedule A row to the bar in the chart.

Source data

The dataset is the FEC Individual Contributions files (Schedule A, itcont.txt from indivYY.zip) released through the FEC bulk data portal at fec.gov/data/browse-data. We use the cycles 2008, 2012, 2016, 2020, 2024, and 2026. Midterm cycles (2010, 2014, 2018, 2022) are not included — this is a limitation of the upstream extraction run, not a deliberate choice. The 2026 cycle is still in progress.

Committee party labels come from the FEC Committee Master file (cm.txt). ActBlue and WinRed conduit committees are mapped to DEM and REP respectively; recipient committees with no clear party flag fall into OTH.

The starting universe is a 1.7 GB intermediate CSV that pre-filters Schedule A rows where the donor's employer field contains a higher-ed term (e.g. UNIVERSITY, COLLEGE, INSTITUTE OF TECHNOLOGY, SCHOOL OF MEDICINE) or matches a known higher-ed alias (MIT, CALTECH, UCLA, etc.). This means: every row analyzed here is from a donor who self-reported a university or college as their employer.

What counts as a "professor"

The challenge: there is no FEC field for "is this person a professor." The occupation field is a free-text self-report. We use it directly, with a curated inclusion list. Each term below is justified individually.

Why not stricter?

A stricter "tenure-track only" filter would miss the 28K-row LECTURER population, adjuncts, postdocs, and emeritus faculty — all of whom are part of the academic labor market and politically active. The dashboard explicitly trades a small false-positive rate (a research staff member or an "academic advisor" misclassified as faculty) for completeness on the labor-market side.

Faculty rank classification

Each donation is assigned exactly one rank, using the donor's normalized occupation string. Order matters: the first matching rule wins, so ASSISTANT PROFESSOR is matched as Assistant before falling through to the generic Full Professor rule on PROFESSOR. 

Emeritus         ← EMERITUS, EMERITA, PROF EMERITUS
Adjunct          ← ADJUNCT
Assistant        ← ASSISTANT PROFESSOR, ASST PROF
Associate        ← ASSOCIATE PROFESSOR, ASSOC PROF
Lecturer         ← LECTURER
Instructor       ← INSTRUCTOR
Postdoc          ← POSTDOCTORAL, POST DOC, POSTDOC
Research Faculty ← RESEARCH PROFESSOR, RESEARCH SCIENTIST, PRINCIPAL INVESTIGATOR
Full Professor   ← PROFESSOR, FULL PROFESSOR, UNIVERSITY PROFESSOR, DISTINGUISHED PROFESSOR
Other Faculty    ← FACULTY, ACADEMIC, PROF (catch-all)

The Full Professor bucket catches anyone who self-describes as PROFESSOR without a more specific rank prefix — this is by far the largest group, and includes both true full-rank professors and donors who simply omit their rank. The chart's Full Professor bar should be read as "self-described professor, no rank specified."

University normalization

The donor's employer field is free-text. "Stanford University", "Stanford U.", "Stanford Department of Economics", "Stanford GSB", and "Stanford Medical School" all appear separately in the raw data. We collapse these to a single canonical name Stanford University using a curated lookup table of 150 institutions: all R1 research universities, the Ivies and peers, prominent liberal arts colleges, well-known professional schools, and a sample of HBCUs and minority-serving institutions.

The matching rule is:

  1. Uppercase the employer string and strip non-alphanumeric characters.
  2. For each canonical institution, check whether any of its associated patterns appears as a whole phrase (with word boundaries) in the normalized string.
  3. The first match wins. Patterns are ordered most-specific first, so UC BERKELEY is checked before any plain BERKELEY pattern would be.

A donation whose employer doesn't match any of the 150 canonicals is bucketed into Other higher-ed (not in top 150). In the current data, this represents 39% of all professor donations and ~$84M in dollars — the long tail of US higher education (community colleges, regional state schools, religious institutions, smaller liberal arts colleges, professional schools outside the top 150). This bucket is excluded from the "Top 30 Universities" chart but included in cycle, rank, state, and donor aggregates.

Donor "lean" classification

The "Lean" column in the top-donors table follows this rule:

  1. Sum each donor's lifetime DEM dollars and REP dollars.
  2. Let partisan = DEM + REP. If partisan == 0, lean is OTH.
  3. If DEM ≥ 2 × REP and DEM / partisan ≥ 0.667, lean is DEM.
  4. Mirror rule for REP.
  5. Otherwise lean is OTH.

This is a conservative donor-level label that puts mixed, bipartisan, and trade-association givers in OTH.

What the dashboard does not do

  • No name disambiguation. A professor who appears at two institutions during the period is counted twice — once at each.
  • No earmark resolution. Earmark targets recorded in FEC memo fields are not used to redirect contributions; we use the recorded recipient committee. Some ActBlue contributions appear as DEM rather than being attributed to the specific candidate.
  • No K-12 inclusion. Higher-ed only.
  • No undergraduate or graduate student inclusion.
  • No verification of occupation. Self-reported text only.
  • No PAC or non-individual contributions. Schedule A only — itemized individual donations to federal committees.

Reproducibility

# 1. Run the upstream university-employee FEC extraction (already done):
#    Uni Political Donations/Uni Political Donations/scripts/university_employee_donations.py

# 2. Run the dashboard prep:
python3 scripts/extract_professor_donations.py

# 3. Build the dashboard:
npm install
npm run build

The aggregated CSVs in public/data/ total under 250 KB and are regenerable from the raw 1.7 GB file in under three minutes on a modern laptop. The full source code is at github.com/banksianr/Professor-Political-Donations.
